Moles

2010-11-03

I was learning about moles today, and I couldn’t help but find it all a little bit confusing. It didn’t really help that my book wasn’t too clear on the difference between Avagadro’s number and a mole, but I think I’ve got it, and shall attempt to explain.

Basically, “a mole of” is a short way of saying 6.02 \times 10^{23}.

That’s pretty much it.

Some sources try to clarify it by saying that it’s the number of molecules in a mass equal to the atomic mass of the thing in grams, but I find that terribly confusing.

Just remember that it’s a short way of saying a certain number. It’s a bit like Mega - it’s not really a unit, it just multiplies whatever comes after it by 10^6. So one mole of atoms is 6.02 \times 10^{23} atoms, one mole of flying saucers is 6.02 \times 10^{23} flying saucers, and one mole of dogs is 6.02 \times 10^{23} dogs.

Things get a bit confusing when people say that Avagadro’s number is 6.02 \times 10^{23}, but it has a unit of mol^{-1}. What the hell is a unit of mol^{-1}? Well, if we just remember that a mole isn’t a real unit, but a shortcut for saying a big number, it turns out that the unit of Avagadro’s number is the inverse of some dimensionless number, so Avagadro’s number itself is dimensionless.

To sum up, the mole isn’t a real unit, it’s just a shortcut for saying a 12-syllable number. So don’t let any fancy-pants chemists confuse you by treating it like a real unit.

Of course, if I’m wrong and there are any fancy-pants chemists who wish to correct me, please do so - I know very little about this, it’s just my take.
